## Global build notes (read first)

- **Role of the page.** narrativestack.ai is the workshop. drrodberger.com is the doorway. This page introduces the thinking, then routes the visitor into the free Quick Leadership Assessment, the single conversion hinge.
- **The assessment, wired like CultureCon.** The mockup and the build must account for the Quick Leadership Assessment exactly as the CultureCon landing page did: the live embed, the email capture, and the zone routing in place, not a placeholder box. Match that proven pattern.
- **Sub-pages for depth.** Anything too specific for the main flow lives one click away on its own page, reached by a button. The Oslo case study is the first. Future case files follow the same pattern, so the main page stays clean.
- **Behind the curtain.** Tease the existence and spirit of the Knockout questions. Never print them on the page. They belong to the Source step of the engagement.
- **Palette.** Deep navy ground, cyan accent, a restrained coral detail. Generous white space. Less is more.
- **Type.** An editorial display face for the big statements, a clean sans for body. Let short lines breathe.
- **One CTA, repeated.** The primary action everywhere is *Take the Quick Leadership Assessment.* The page opens and closes on it.
- **Cross-link, do not fork.** One line frames drrodberger.com as where you meet the person, never a separate company.

## Section 2 — The Premise

**Purpose:** Name the phenomenon. Open the gap. Land a setup question that hangs.

**Heading:** The Story You Did Not Write

**Copy:**
Leaders are always telling a story. The question is whether they know it.

Not the story in the mission statement. Not the one the communications team polished before the quarterly update. The actual story. The one your decisions tell, the behaviors you model, the voices you amplify and the ones you let go quiet.

In most organizations there is a gap between the story a leader intends and the story their people actually receive. That gap is where good people leave, where trust thins, where the best thinking stops showing up in the room.

Most leaders never look at it. The ones who do change what it is like to work for them.

**Pull quote:** What story are you actually telling right now?

**Build note:** Editorial spacing. The pull quote sits alone with air around it.

## Section 3 — Authored vs Assigned

**Purpose:** Name the distinction at the heart of the work, simply, before any framework arrives. This is the emotional core and the gentlest on-ramp.

**Heading:** Authored, or Assigned

**Copy:**
There are two kinds of stories you carry. An Authored story is one you have examined, claimed, and chosen to live inside. An Assigned story is one that was handed to you, often before you had any say in it.

Most professional lives run almost entirely on Assigned stories. So do most companies.

The work begins the moment you can tell which is which. Then it asks a harder thing of you. It asks you to take the pen back.

**Build note:** Capitalize Authored and Assigned as the named distinction, with the cyan accent on each, matching the deck. Keep this section short and quiet. It should feel like a hand on the shoulder, not a lecture.

## Section 4 — Two Ways to Speak

**Purpose:** Introduce the first framework. Name the shadow on default leadership communication.

**Heading:** Kitchen Table, Not Police Report

**Copy:**
A leader can speak in two registers. The Police Report is accurate, verifiable, and forgettable. It states what happened and asks nothing of anyone. The Kitchen Table is the register people lean toward. It carries the weight of a real story, told by a person willing to be known.

Most leadership communication defaults to the Police Report, then wonders why no one believes it. The work here moves you toward the Kitchen Table without losing the room.

**Build note:** Two-panel layout, Kitchen Table and Police Report side by side. Capitalize both terms exactly.

## Section 5 — The Method

**Purpose:** Give the thinking intellectual structure. Establish that this is a reading, not a vibe.

**Heading:** A Reading, Not a Verdict

**Copy:**
This is not a personality test, and it is not spin. The Narrative Stack is a reading of how your story is actually working.

Four Quadrants, the rooms of a life: Professional, Personal, Passions, Pursuits. Four pillars a story has to pass: Coherence, Conviction, Resonance, Legibility. Sixteen places where a story either holds or quietly costs you something.

The work runs on one spine. Source the stories you have never been asked for. Examine which ones you authored and which were assigned to you before you could choose. Author the version you can carry into the room that matters.

**Pull quote / triad graphic:** Source. Examine. Author.

**Build note:** Use the Stack cubes motif and the three-part Source · Examine · Author band. Frameworks capitalized exactly. Keep the copy tight. This section sells substance, not length.

## Section 8 — The Five Zones

**Purpose:** Set the expectation of a tailored result. Keep every zone hopeful, none a grade.

**Heading:** Where Your Story Stands

**Copy (five cards):**
- **Story At Risk.** The gaps have started to fill themselves in, with versions you did not write.
- **Story Emerging.** You have begun to treat story as a leadership tool. It is real, and still fragile.
- **Story Drift.** You have strong moments and no steady spine. On a good day it lands.
- **Story Developing.** The pieces are there. You are ready to codify it and deploy it on purpose.
- **Story Aligned.** The story is clear and credible across the organization. Now you keep it sharp as things change.

None of these is a grade. Each one points to different work.

**Build note:** Five cards, cyan accents, equal weight. These map to the five result pages and videos behind the assessment.

# Sub-page — Case File: Oslo, 2024

**Reached by:** the "Read the case file" button in Section 10. Off the main flow on purpose.

**Title:** The Story That Reached Davos Before the Data Did

**Copy:**
A few years ago I stood backstage in Oslo, about to interview the founder of a company that had flirted with unicorn status and then nearly gone bankrupt. He was nervous. I was stressed. I could feel the standard interviewer-and-guest dance setting in, the kind that produces nothing worth listening to.

So I did the thing I now do almost by reflex. I set the barrier aside and went first. I told him about the death of my own company, and what the slow aftermath felt like, the parts I have rarely told anyone.

He looked at me and said, you understand me.

What he told me next, he had told almost no one. The panic. The fear. What he came to name as the aftermath of the near-collapse. Within weeks of that conversation reaching the world, he had secured the investment that turned the company around.

I did not write his story. I sourced it. I examined it with him in real time. I helped him author a version he could put back into the world. That is the work, and it is the same work the assessment starts you on.
